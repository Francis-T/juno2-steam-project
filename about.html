Secure and Trustworthy Framework for Integrated Energy and Mobility in Smart Connected Communities

About the Project

Internet of Things (IoT), Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS), and Data Sciences are fueling the development of innovative solutions for various applications in smart connected communities (SCC). These solutions are typically data-driven, thus making them vulnerable to data integrity attacks. Some solutions leveraging human as active agents may be susceptible to selfish behavior due to incentive gap between individual and social objectives in SCC.

At the same time, the exponential increase in velocity and volume of data has created a bottleneck for computing resources in the communities. This has catalyzed the growth in shared computing paradigms, such as edge/fog computing. The shared nature of computing resources makes the data integrity attacks even more likely, thereby reducing trust in decision quality of SCC applications, such as smart mobility and smart energy. Additionally, the increasing dependence on dynamic data-driven support systems implies that any operational breach in the underlying SCC networks or services due to attacks or failures will have cascading effects. Lastly, such attacks will not only cripple the SCC operations but also escalate customers' privacy concerns influencing the extent to which they are willing to share data. This in turn will affect the quantity of information required to make robust decisions, making trustworthiness in SCC applications very challenging.

Objectives

This collaborative proposal between US and Japan, called STEAM, aims to develop novel integrated frameworks to address important security and trustworthiness challenges in two emerging SCC applications -- smart mobility and energy. In particular, considering various threat models, we propose innovative solutions to five inter-related research thrusts:

  • Secure and trustworthy decision making under uncertainty, by developing efficient anomaly detection and trust models;
  • Integrating privacy-preserving computations with secure and trustworthy decision making schemes by adopting fully homomorphic encryption (FHE);
  • Characterizing trade-offs between security, privacy and available resources;
  • Developing a secure and trustworthy middleware architecture as applied to smart mobility and energy, that incorporates privacy-preserving algorithms, resource constraints, and trustworthiness of data sources and decision making schemes; and
  • Evaluating the architecture and proposed solutions with exemplar case study applications.

Intellectual Merit

The novelty of the STEAM project lies in the integrated approach to handling security and trustworthiness of two interdependent SCC applications, in the domain of smart mobility and energy, by developing innovative privacy-preserving FHE compatible algorithms and models for anomaly detection, trust and reputation scoring, such that privacy is not sacrificed for the sake of security. Additionally, we study trade-offs between security, privacy, trust levels, resources, and performance. Our proposed secure middleware architecture makes FHE computations and trustworthy decision making available to the application services. Our hypothesis is that if we are successful, then we will see the next big step in reliable privacy-preserving data-driven decision support systems, which are required if we want to encourage active participation of customers in the data economy of SCC.

Broader Impacts

The STEAM project will not only have tremendous scientific impacts on securing SCC applications including smart mobility and energy, but also on CPS and IoT. It will further provide a unique platform for interdisciplinary education, training, and experiential learning for undergraduate and graduate students across five partner institutions in the US and Japan. The PIs propose to jointly develop and offer a new course on "Securing Smart and Connected Communities" via distance education. This course will emphasize hands-on security projects with lab exercises designed around smart mobility and energy testbeds. Due to societal relevance and team-oriented collaboration approach, the STEAM project will help attract women and underrepresented minority students. Moreover, smart vehicles and IoT applications will encourage K-12 students to participate in summer camps organized at partner universities. Research findings and project outcomes will be widely disseminated via a dedicated website, invited talks, tutorial and seminar presentations.
